Florence Cathedral

Florence, Italy  ·  Completed 1436
1 / 4
Overview

The Cathedral that Defined an Era

The Cattedrale di Santa Maria del Fiore is the cathedral church of Florence. Its construction began in 1296 in the Gothic style to the design of Arnolfo di Cambio. The building's defining feature — its enormous octagonal dome — was not added until the 15th century, when Filippo Brunelleschi solved an engineering problem that had stumped builders for over a century.

Brunelleschi's dome, completed in 1436, has an inner diameter of 43.7 metres and rises to 91 metres. It was built without a temporary wooden supporting framework, using an innovative herringbone brickwork pattern and a double-shell construction method.

Who Built It
Filippo Brunelleschi (1377 – 1446)

Brunelleschi was commissioned to design the dome in 1418 after winning a public competition. Trained originally as a goldsmith, he had no formal architectural training — yet proposed a revolutionary double-shell dome built without temporary wooden centering, a method no one had used since antiquity. He oversaw construction personally for over 14 years until the dome's completion in 1436, inventing new machinery and techniques along the way.

Architectural Features

A Revolution in Structure

The dome's double-shell design consists of an inner and outer shell connected by stone ribs. The herringbone brick pattern distributes load without centring. Eight white marble ribs are visible from outside, matched by sixteen internal hidden ribs. At the top, a marble lantern weighing over 700 tonnes locks the structure in compression.

Significance

The Florence Cathedral dome proved that an ancient structural challenge could be solved through ingenuity alone. It became the direct inspiration for St. Peter's in Rome, the Panthéon in Paris, and the US Capitol dome. Brunelleschi's methods established the template for Renaissance and Neoclassical architecture worldwide.
