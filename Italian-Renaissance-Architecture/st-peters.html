St. Peter's Basilica

Vatican City  ·  Completed 1626

Overview

The World's Greatest Basilica

St. Peter's Basilica in Vatican City is the largest church in the world and one of the holiest sites in Christendom. Construction of the current building began in 1506 under Pope Julius II, replacing an older 4th-century basilica. The project took over 120 years and involved the greatest architects of the Renaissance and Baroque periods.

The building is crowned by Michelangelo's dome — 136 metres high and visible from across Rome. Bernini's colonnaded piazza, added in 1656–67, frames the approach with 284 columns in four rows. The basilica's interior nave stretches 218 metres and can hold 60,000 worshippers.

Who Built It
Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475 – 1564)

Michelangelo was appointed chief architect of St. Peter's in 1547 by Pope Paul III — at the age of 71. He accepted the commission without pay, considering it a service to God. He redesigned the crossing piers, simplified Bramante's complex plan, and designed the iconic dome. Though he died in 1564 before the dome was built, his wooden model was followed closely by Giacomo della Porta, who completed it in 1590.

Architectural Features

Renaissance Meets Baroque

Michelangelo's dome is a double-shell structure inspired by Brunelleschi's Florence Cathedral dome. It sits on a tall drum pierced by large windows and is supported on four massive internal piers. The facade by Maderno features giant Corinthian pilasters. Inside, Bernini's bronze baldachin — 29 metres tall — marks the tomb of St. Peter directly beneath the dome.

Significance

St. Peter's is the culmination of Renaissance architecture — the point where the movement's ideals were pushed to their grandest expression. Michelangelo's dome became the direct model for the Panthéon in Paris, St. Paul's Cathedral in London, and the US Capitol. The building defined monumental civic and religious design for centuries.
