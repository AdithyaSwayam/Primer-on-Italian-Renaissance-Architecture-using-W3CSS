Palazzo Medici Riccardi

Florence, Italy  ·  Completed 1484
1 / 4
Overview

The Model for the Renaissance Palace

The Palazzo Medici Riccardi in Florence was built for Cosimo de' Medici, the most powerful banker and political figure in Florence. Designed by Michelozzo di Bartolomeo and begun in 1444, it was the Medici family residence until 1540 and established the typology of the Renaissance urban palace.

The building is organised around a central courtyard with an open arcade on the ground floor. The facade is notable for its three clearly distinct storeys — each using progressively smoother stonework from rough rusticated blocks at ground level to smooth ashlar at the top — all crowned by a large classical cornice.

Who Built It
Michelozzo di Bartolomeo (1396 – 1472)

Michelozzo was the Medici family's trusted architect and one of Cosimo de' Medici's closest associates. Cosimo chose him over Brunelleschi for this commission — reportedly because Brunelleschi's design was deemed too grand and would attract envy. Michelozzo was tasked in 1444 with creating a palazzo that projected wealth and authority while remaining within the conventions of a private residence, basing his design on careful study of ancient Roman buildings.

Architectural Features

Rustication, Rhythm, and Order

The facade's most distinctive feature is its three-tier stone treatment. The ground floor uses large, deeply rough-cut rusticated blocks. The middle floor uses smoother, lightly worked stone. The upper floor is finished in flat ashlar. This gradation from massive to refined became the defining grammar of the Italian palazzo facade.

Significance

The Palazzo Medici established the formal model for urban palace design followed across Italy for two centuries. Its influence can be seen in the Palazzo Rucellai, the Palazzo Strozzi, and ultimately across European urbanism. It demonstrated how private domestic architecture could express civic authority and cultural aspiration.
