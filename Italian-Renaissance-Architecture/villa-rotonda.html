Villa Rotonda

Vicenza, Italy  ·  Completed 1592
1 / 4
Overview

Perfect Symmetry on a Hillside

The Villa Almerico Capra, universally known as La Rotonda, is a Renaissance villa just outside Vicenza in the Veneto region of Italy. Designed by Andrea Palladio around 1565 and completed after his death in 1592, it is perhaps the most intellectually influential building in the history of Western architecture.

The villa's plan is a perfect square with a circular hall at its centre, capped by a low dome. On each of its four facades, an identical Ionic portico — six columns supporting a triangular pediment — opens views in all four directions, making it equally elegant from any approach.

Who Built It
Andrea Palladio (1508 – 1580)

Palladio designed the Villa Rotonda around 1565 for Paolo Almerico, a retired papal official who wanted a hilltop retreat with views in all directions. Palladio used the villa as a demonstration of his principles — perfect symmetry, classical orders applied rationally, a building integrated with its landscape. He died in 1580 and the villa was completed by his student Vincenzo Scamozzi in 1592.

Architectural Features

Four Porticos, One Vision

Each of the four identical porticos frames a different view of the Vicenza landscape — the villa functions as a belvedere as much as a residence. The Ionic columns are precisely proportioned per Palladio's own rules. The central circular room opens to the sky through the dome's oculus, serving as the spatial and conceptual heart of the building.

Significance

La Rotonda is arguably the single most copied building in history. Its influence on British Palladianism — Chiswick House, Mereworth Castle — and on American architecture — Jefferson's Monticello, the Virginia State Capitol — is direct and documented. It transformed the idea of what a private house could aspire to be.
